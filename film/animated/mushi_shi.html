Mushi-shi

On of the more prominent belief systems seen in Mushi-shi is Shinto ideas and values. This can be seen in the explanation of mushi. The mushi are seen as the closest beings to the origins of life itself. This reflects the Shinto beliefs in sacred nature. The mushi are a natural part of nature and are sacred as they are everywhere and are powerful beings for heir close connection to life. Hence, there are kami-like in this respect and should be dealt with in some way. For instance, in episode 22 “Shrine in the Sea”, the villagers offering up their loved ones so they can be reborn is almost like a sacrificial ceremony to the mushi in the dragon’s palace underwater. If they are failed to be dealt with, these mushi, like kami, can cause trouble for people. This can be seen in episode 3 “Tender Horns”, where the people left the Un and Ah to their own devices which caused them to start preying on the villagers by damaging their hearing.

Furthermore, these mushi are very powerful entities similar to kami. As stated before, they can do things such as eating noises or allowing people to be reborn again. They can also give sight to people that have lost them. In episode 25 “Eye of Fortune, Eye of Misfortune”, the blind women was able to see once she saw the powerful, rare mushi. However, the mushi also gave her many other powers including the ability inhumanly well and the ability to see the future.

There are also values and beliefs from Buddhism that are reflected in the material directly or indirectly. In Mushi-shi, the influence from the values of Buddhism can be seen in the concept of umiaoshi from episode 22. The ability of the mushi to enable people to be given a new life again harkens back to the ideas of reincarnation and rebirth. There is still the same person and soul.
