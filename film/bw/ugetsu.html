In the film Ugestu, the influence of Buddhism is obvious in the priest coming to enlighten Genjuro of the reality of the mansion. The Buddhist priest speaks of the karma in not letting him go without helping him. As a monk, it is his responsibility to help. As well, some of the motifs and practices of Buddhism are also seen in the film. There is the use of Buddhist prayers written on Genjuro’s body. This acted as a ward and protection for him against the yurei that were deceiving him. Another practice reflecting Buddhist values are the references to the buddha throughout the film from ordinary people. Often characters have said “Merciful Buddha” in exclamation or reaction to horrible events such as seeing the man died after he was pillaged and attacked by pirates.

The values and beliefs of Bushido are also apparent in the movie. Tobei had ambitions to become a samurai. When he finally received his status by dubious means, he finally achieves this rank for his courage in killing a high-level general. He later in the film launches into a long tangent on what it takes to become a great samurai:

“First of all, a head, meaning knowledge. Then an arm, meaning strength. And then the art of war, meaning deeds. And then to take advantage of the occasion in flight, like a hawk. With an overview of the great Chinese tactics, nothing can go wrong.... Tactics: The Koshu school. Firearms: Karamaru school. Spear: Otsubo school. Then we have fortification, e.g. how to build fortresses. After that the art of defence with offensive and defensive formations...”1
Thus, from this quotation, Tobei lists indirectly the values of Bushido. For instance, there is the stress on all the tactics that one should know and different weapons as well as schools to study. Tobei advises that to be a strong samurai one should be skilled and strong along with being disciplined in their all their efforts which are part of the Bushido values and beliefs.
